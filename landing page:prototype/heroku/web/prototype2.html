    Etan Patz’s Mother Speaks in Court of the Day He Disappeared

    By JAMES C. McKINLEY Jr.FEB. 2, 2015

    Inside
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story Share This Page
      Continue reading the main story

      She walked steadily through the courtroom, a lifetime of accomplishments and lasting tragedy behind her, a witness stand before her.

      Julie Patz began with simple memories, of her son’s trusting personality, of how he loved to be photographed, of how life was back then in SoHo, a gritty artists’ colony in 1979. And then she described the heartbreaking event that has defined much of her life and identity: the disappearance of that son, Etan.

      She was one of the last people to have seen the boy, then 6, on the day he disappeared. She sent him off to school with a tote bag full of Matchbox cars and a dollar that he had earned, for a soda. Some memories had blurred with time; others were painfully clear.

      “I watched him walk one block away,” she said on Monday in State Supreme Court in Manhattan, at the trial of Pedro Hernandez, who has been charged with Etan’s kidnapping and murder. “I turned around to go back upstairs, and that was the last time I saw him.”

      The dollar bill, she remembered precisely, was in Etan’s left hand. She was composed through most of her testimony, even laughing at memories of her boy’s burning desire to be grown up, to go to school on his own. But as she recounted the numbing terror she felt when she learned Etan had not made it to school, she paused. “This is not easy,” she said, dabbing tears from her eyes. His body was never found.

      Though Julie Patz is now 72, she will always be known as Etan’s mother, recalled for her loss and for what she made of it. In the years after his disappearance, she and her husband, Stanley, led a political movement that raised awareness about missing children, and helped pass laws that created a national legal framework for such cases.

      Over the last 15 years, Ms. Patz has largely withdrawn from the public eye and let her husband take the lead in keeping the investigation alive. But on Monday, she re-emerged to testify about the day her son disappeared.

      Mr. Hernandez, 54, a factory worker from Maple Shade, N.J., worked as a clerk in the corner bodega where Etan was headed to buy a soft drink. He was arrested in May 2012 after his brother-in-law told the police about stories circulating in the Hernandez family, stories in which Mr. Hernandez had talked about killing a child in New York City in the late 1970s.

      Continue reading the main story

      After a long interrogation, Mr. Hernandez gave the authorities two videotaped confessions. In both, he said he had lured Etan to the basement of the bodega with the promise of a soda, and strangled him there.

      Now he denies the charges. His lawyers say his confessions are false, the inventions of a weak mind after hours of police interrogation. They say Mr. Hernandez not only has an I.Q. of 70, but also has a personality disorder, making it hard for him to distinguish fantasy from reality.

      Defense lawyers also intend to argue the evidence gathered over three decades points to another man: Jose A. Ramos, a convicted child molester who was a longtime suspect in the case.

      In her testimony on Monday, Ms. Patz recalled the gray morning of May 25, 1979, when she last saw Etan. She did not look at Mr. Hernandez as she spoke to the jury. He sat placidly at the defense table, staring straight ahead with no reaction to what she said.

      Continue reading the main story

      “He was insisting he was very grown up and he could take the money he had earned to buy his own drink,” she said of Etan. “I capitulated and said O.K., you can walk to the school bus by yourself.”

      The tote bag, she recalled, was made of navy blue cloth, decorated with white circus elephants. Along with the Matchbox cars, it held a lunch and a cardboard tube packed with pencils, she said. He was wearing a cap from Eastern Airlines.

      “I told him to go straight to the bodega and get his drink, so he wouldn’t miss his bus,” she recalled.

      Ms. Patz said the corner bodega where Mr. Hernandez worked was a crammed, dark, dirty and poorly stocked store, where her son liked to go to get sodas and candy. “Anything sweet,” she said. Still, she recalled that she and other parents told their children to go into the bodega in case of an emergency.

      She said the morning he disappeared was busy. A neighbor had left her 2-year-old daughter with the Patzes, which meant Ms. Patz was caring for two toddlers: the girl and her 2-year-old son, Ari. Her 8-year-old, Shira, was also home, having declined to go to school.

      Ms. Patz was preparing for the arrival of the toddlers and young children enrolled in the day care center that she had started in the loft in the early 1970s. Etan, she said, was lobbying her to walk to the school bus stop on his own because his big sister was staying home, she said.

      Etan was an extremely sensitive, trusting child, who took scoldings to heart and did not need to be disciplined often, Ms. Patz testified, smiling as she recalled him.

      “Totally outgoing and trusting of everyone,” she said. “Totally nonjudgmental about people. Everyone who he had met once was his friend and was a nice person.”

      Ms. Patz had not spoken publicly about her son’s disappearance in years. In the early 1980s, she became a driving force in the missing-child movement. She and her husband successfully pushed, along with the parents of other abducted children, for missing-child reports to be entered into the national crime database of the Federal Bureau of Investigation. Etan was one of the first missing children whose faces were depicted on a milk carton, and the date of his disappearance, May 25, became National Missing Children’s Day.

      The prosecutor, Joan Illuzzi-Orbon, asked Ms. Patz several questions about an incident the day before Etan was abducted, when he rode off on a Big Wheel and was missing for several minutes. Ms. Patz recalled running downstairs, her heart pounding, and searching frantically for him before he reappeared.

      “I screamed at him and chastised him severely for giving me a heart attack,” she remembered.

      During cross-examination, Harvey Fishbein, one of Mr. Hernandez’s lawyers, focused many of his questions on that incident. He suggested Ms. Patz had told the police she had sent Etan to the bodega to retrieve his lunchbox, and had also mentioned to detectives that Etan had met a grown-up stranger while riding his tricycle around the block.

      Continue reading the main story

      “Sir, it’s all a very long time ago,” she said. “I don’t recall these details.”

      The defense lawyer also focused on Susan Harrington, a woman who dated Mr. Ramos. She was hired in May 1979 to walk Etan and other children home from school. Ms. Patz acknowledged she had met Ms. Harrington in Washington Square Park with a man fitting Mr. Ramos’s description.

      Another witness, Chelsea Altman, a childhood friend of Etan’s, testified that Ms. Harrington sometimes met a man when she walked her and Etan home, and the man accompanied them. But she denied the man, who had red hair, looked like Mr. Ramos.

      Under questioning from Mr. Fishbein, Ms. Altman, 42, said Etan had talked in the days before he vanished about wanting to go away with a friend named Johnny, who he said had a van and candy, though she said she thought it was an imaginary friend.

      During Ms. Patz’s testimony, she became emotional as she recounted how some women in the neighborhood had criticized her for laughing and playing with her other children several months after Etan went missing. They had suggested she was responsible for what had happened, she said.

      Over time, she said, Etan’s disappearance was particularly hard on her other children. Shira was briefly a suspect, she said. Ari moved to Hawaii, in part to escape the news media’s attention.

      “You continually try to regain a sense of normalcy in your daily life and then have it tugged from under you,” Ms. Patz said.

      “For my daughter and my son, this has been almost their entire lives,” she said. “It’s been very, very, very hard.”
